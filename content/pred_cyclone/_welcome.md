**Superforecasting challenge — Atlantic Cyclone 2026**

Below is a structured reasoning trace and an opening probability estimate for the question:

> **Will at least one named tropical cyclone form in the Atlantic Ocean before July 15, 2026?**

---

## Reasoning Trace

**Step 1 — Base rate**

The climatological median date for the first Atlantic named storm is around **June 20** — 25 days before our July 15 threshold. Historically, roughly **85–90% of seasons** produce at least one named storm before July 15. The trend has also been toward earlier seasons: from 2015 through 2024, only two of ten seasons had no storm before June 1.

**Step 2 — 2026 season-specific factors**

*Pushing probability down:*
- A **strong El Niño** is developing — possibly the strongest on record. El Niño dramatically increases vertical wind shear over the tropical Atlantic, which tears apart developing storms before they can organize.
- Wind shear in 2026 is projected as the **second-highest since 1981** (behind only 2015).
- NOAA forecasts a **below-normal season** (8–14 named storms vs. the ~14 average), with 55% probability of below-normal.

*Keeping probability up:*
- Even strong El Niño years see **early-season storms** — in 2015 (a record El Niño), Tropical Storm Ana formed April 19.
- Below-normal season ≠ late season: below-normal means fewer storms total, not necessarily a delayed start.
- Atlantic sea surface temperatures remain **near or slightly above average**, providing thermodynamic fuel especially in the Gulf of Mexico and western Caribbean — areas less exposed to El Niño shear.
- No storm has formed yet (as of late May), but the season does not officially start until June 1.

**Step 3 — Key analogue: 2009**

The clearest historical precedent for the downside scenario is **2009**: a moderate El Niño year with only 9 named storms, where the first storm (Ana) did not form until August 15. If 2026 produces a comparable or stronger El Niño suppression, a similar delay is plausible.

**Step 4 — Synthesis**

- High base rate (~87% historically) anchors the estimate firmly above 50%.
- El Niño is a meaningful downside risk, but not enough to override the base rate — most El Niño seasons still produce early storms (2015, 2014, 2013, 2006 all resolved YES).
- The 2009 scenario is real but uncommon even in suppressed years.

*85% probability that at least one named tropical cyclone forms in the Atlantic before July 15, 2026.*
