**Superforecasting challenge — Atlantic Cyclone 2026**

Below is a structured reasoning trace and an opening probability estimate for the question:

> **Will at least one named tropical cyclone form in the Atlantic Ocean before July 15, 2026?**

---

## Reasoning Trace

**Step 1 — Base rate**

The climatological median date for the first Atlantic named storm is around **June 20** — 25 days before our July 15 threshold. Historically, roughly **85–90% of seasons** produce at least one named storm before July 15. The trend has been toward earlier seasons: from 2015 through 2025, only two of eleven seasons had no storm before June 1, and the two most recent first storms (Alberto, Jun 17 2024; Andrea, Jun 23 2025) both formed comfortably before mid-July.

**Step 2 — 2026 season-specific factors**

*Pushing probability down:*

- A **developing El Niño** with 98% probability and 80% chance of moderate-to-strong intensity — possibly among the strongest on record. El Niño dramatically increases vertical wind shear over the tropical Atlantic, tearing apart developing storms before they can organize.
- Wind shear in 2026 is projected as the **second-highest since 1981** (behind only 2015).
- NOAA forecasts a **below-normal season** (8–14 named storms vs. the ~14 average), with 55% probability of below-normal.
- As of May 26, **no storm has formed and the NHC seven-day outlook shows no expected formation through ~June 1**. The two tropical waves currently in the basin are not expected to develop in the Atlantic.

*Keeping probability up:*

- Even strong El Niño years see **early-season storms**. In the historical analogues, four of five El Niño years (1997, 2006, 2015, 2023) produced a named storm before July 15; only 2009 did not.
- Below-normal season ≠ late season: fewer storms total does not necessarily mean a delayed start.
- Atlantic SSTs remain **near or slightly above average**, providing thermodynamic fuel — especially in the Gulf of Mexico and western Caribbean, regions less exposed to El Niño shear.
- Models suggest a **stationary frontal boundary** may set up off the southeastern U.S. coast into the Gulf around the first week of June — historically a common trigger for early-season Gulf development.
- The threshold gives a **seven-week window** (May 26 → July 15). The climatological probability of *no* storm forming over such a long window is low even under suppressed conditions.

**Step 3 — Key downside analogue: 2009**

The clearest historical precedent for the downside scenario is **2009**: a moderate El Niño year with only 9 named storms, in which the first storm (Ana) did not form until August 15. If 2026 produces El Niño suppression *at least as severe* as 2009 and the early-June Gulf setup fails to spin anything up, a comparable delay is plausible.

**Step 4 — Synthesis**

- High base rate (~87% historically) anchors the estimate firmly above 50%.
- El Niño is a meaningful downside risk, but the strongest analogues (1997, 2015) still produced first storms well before July 15. Only 2009 — one moderate-El-Niño year out of five — resolved NO.
- The current quiet outlook through early June is a *small* negative update; it removes the chance of a late-May storm but leaves six full weeks for development.
- The possible early-June Gulf frontal boundary is a *small* positive update.

These two near-term updates roughly cancel. The dominant inputs remain the historical base rate and the El Niño analogues.

*86% probability that at least one named tropical cyclone forms in the Atlantic before July 15, 2026.*
